03 · Buyer Messaging / Problem statements

Problem statements for enterprise buyers

Name the leak before you name the product. These are the five problems BRG exists to make visible — written as the buyer would describe them on an incident review.

No attribution

"The inference bill went up 40% last quarter and no one can tell me which team or feature drove it." Cost exists; ownership doesn't.

Premium-model overuse

The most expensive model is the default for everything, including requests a smaller model would answer identically. Nobody is measuring the gap.

Duplicate inference

Identical or near-identical requests are paid for again and again because there is no cache at the runtime. Pure waste.

Retry amplification

A single user request becomes three to five billed calls under naive retry logic, especially during provider degradation — and it never shows up cleanly in the bill.

Routing inefficiency

Traffic is pinned to one provider or model by hardcoded clients, so there is no way to shift load on cost, latency, or availability.

The meta-problem

Inference runs through application code, not infrastructure — so the people who own cost and reliability have no control point. BRG is that control point.

03 · Buyer Messaging / Objection handling

Objection handling

The real ones, answered the way a credible engineer would — by conceding what's true and reframing the rest.

Q"Won't an inline gateway add latency to every call?"
Decision overhead is sub-millisecond and cache hits are faster than a provider round-trip. We don't ask you to take that on faith — the assessment measures it on your traffic. If BRG adds latency you can feel, it isn't doing its job.
Q"My provider already shows me usage. Why do I need this?"
Provider dashboards show you spend per provider, after the fact. They can't attribute to your teams and workflows, can't see duplicate or retry-amplified traffic, and can't move load across providers. BRG sits at your runtime, so it sees what the provider can't.
Q"Isn't this just an API gateway with extra steps?"
A generic gateway proxies bytes. BRG understands inference economics — model pricing, token accounting, semantic equivalence, fallback cost. The unit of reasoning is the dollar and the request, not the route. That's the difference between routing and runtime economics.
Q"We'd rather build this internally."
Many teams start there — a proxy and a dashboard. It's the maintenance that gets you: pricing catalogs change weekly, providers drift, caching correctness is subtle, and someone now owns it on call. BRG is that surface, maintained, with an escape hatch if you ever want out.
Q"Is this going to lock me into your routing decisions?"
The opposite. BRG is provider-neutral by design and routing is declarative — you own the policy in Git. Its entire reason to exist is to reduce lock-in to any single provider. You can fail open through it at any time.

07 · Strategy / Strategic moat

Strategic moat narrative

The internal story of why this compounds. Not for the website — for the team and the cap table. It's the reason visibility-first is a strategy, not a limitation.

1
The wedge is placement
Being inline with inference traffic is the position everyone else lacks. Dashboards observe from outside; BRG sits on the path where cost is incurred and reliability is decided. Placement is the asset.
2
Visibility earns the right to act
We sell visibility first because it's the lowest-risk way in — and because once we're the system of record for inference economics, optimization and control are the obvious next steps, on infrastructure they already trust.
3
Data compounds
Every instrumented environment improves our pricing models, routing heuristics, and benchmarks. The economics ledger becomes a dataset no point tool or dashboard can replicate.
4
Switching cost is structural, not contractual
Once routing, caching, attribution, and failover run through BRG as declarative platform infrastructure, it's load-bearing. That's durable in the right way — earned through operational trust, not lock-in.
5
Economics is the on-ramp to control
The runtime that already sees and shapes every inference call is the only natural place to later govern it. We win cost first, then become the control plane — because we're already on the path. That second act is the moat; the first act earns the right to build it.

BRG starts as the cheapest, lowest-risk way to see inference spend — and ends as the control point the whole inference path runs through. Visibility is the wedge, the runtime position is the moat, and the data we accumulate makes both harder to dislodge every quarter.
