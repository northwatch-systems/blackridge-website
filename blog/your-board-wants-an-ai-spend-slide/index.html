Your Board Wants An AI Spend Slide. What Will It Say?

Board AI spend conversations are coming regardless. The difference between a defensible slide and an uncomfortable one is forensic evidence — specific waste classes, root causes, fixes in progress, confidence-gated savings.

At some point in the next board or exec meeting, someone will ask about AI spend. Not as a curiosity. As a line item. The question will not be "how much did we spend on AI?" That answer is on the invoice. The question will be "what did we get for it, and is it under control?" Most engineering teams are not ready for that question. Not because they have been careless. Because the tools in most AI stacks produce invoices, not answers. You can show the total. You can show the trend. You can show which team or model consumed the most. You cannot show which spend was productive, which was waste, what behavior created the waste, and what engineering is doing about it. That gap shows up in the meeting.

The slide that starts the wrong meeting

A slide that says "AI spend increased 34% last quarter" opens a conversation nobody is prepared to finish. Where did it go? What drove the increase? Is this expected? Is this efficient? What are we doing about it?

If the answer to any of those is "we're looking into it" or "it's complex to track," the conversation has already gone badly.

The defensible slide

A slide that says — numbers illustrative — "AI spend increased 34%. $180K was productive inference tied to shipped features. $47K was waste we have identified and are fixing: retry amplification from a tool integration issue and context bloat from a misconfigured retrieval step. Engineering has fixes in the current sprint. Expected savings: $38K monthly ongoing." That is a different meeting.

The difference between those two slides is not effort. It is evidence.

The first slide is what you get from billing and attribution. The second slide is what you get from forensic reconstruction of the workflow economics: which spend was productive, which was waste, what caused the waste, what the fix is, and how confident the estimate is.

Finance can read the second slide. Engineering can defend it. That is a slide worth presenting.

Evidence changes the meeting

Board conversations about AI spend are becoming standard. The teams that walk in with forensic evidence: specific waste classes, root causes, fixes in progress, confidence-gated savings. They will have a different meeting than the teams that walk in with a token chart.

Building the second slide requires looking inside the workflows before the meeting, not after.

We built Blackridge to produce the evidence that makes AI spend defensible to leadership — not just visible to engineering.
