How To Evaluate AI Cost Tools (Including Ours)

AI cost tools operate at four layers: billing, telemetry, attribution, and forensics. Most teams have the first three. The gap is evidence-backed reconstruction of why workflows cost what they cost.

The AI cost tooling market is confusing because everything sounds similar. Gateways say they handle cost. Observability platforms say they handle cost. FinOps dashboards say they handle cost. Attribution tools say they handle cost. They all handle part of the problem. They handle different parts. Knowing which part you are buying matters more than the vendor category on the website. Here is the framework we use. Blackridge fits into it at a specific layer.

The four layers of AI cost tooling

Layer 1: Billing

What you paid: provider invoices, model-level totals, monthly spend.

Every organization has this. It answers: how much did we spend?

Layer 2: Telemetry

What ran: traces, spans, request logs, latency, error rates, token counts.

Observability platforms live here. This layer answers: what technical activity occurred?

Layer 3: Attribution

Where the spend came from: cost allocated to teams, projects, workflows, customers, features, or tags.

FinOps dashboards and tagging systems live here. This layer answers: who or what generated the cost?

Layer 4: Forensics

Why it happened: evidence-backed reconstruction of the economic behavior inside workflows.

Waste classification. Productive vs wasteful spend separation. Confidence-gated savings. Root-cause identification.

This layer answers: why did the spend occur, was it justified, and what should change?

Most organizations have layers 1 through 3 in some form.

The consistent gap is layer 4.

Where existing categories stop

Gateways sit between your application and the model provider. They route traffic, standardize API access, and expose usage metrics. Good gateways create clean data for billing and telemetry. Some offer attribution tagging.

Gateways operate at the request boundary. They see calls passing through.

They usually do not reconstruct what happened across a multi-step workflow. The economic behavior - retries, fallbacks, abandoned branches, duplicate work - emerges from the sequence, not any single request.

Observability platforms give you layer 2 in depth.

Traces, spans, request trees, latency analysis, error tracking. If you need to debug a slow or failing workflow, observability tools are the right answer.

They usually do not attach economics to the trace.

A span can tell you a model call took 1.2 seconds and returned 4,000 tokens. It does not tell you whether that call was productive work or a retry. It does not tell you whether the token count was appropriate or bloated. It does not tell you what the call cost relative to its role in the workflow.

FinOps dashboards give you layer 3.

Cost allocated by team, project, environment, or tag. If you need to know who is spending how much, FinOps tools answer that.

They do not explain why a team's spend is what it is.

Allocation tells you Team A spent $47,000. It does not tell you that part of the spend came from fallback tax, retrieval bloat, retry amplification, or abandoned branches.

What forensics adds

Forensics is layer 4.

It reconstructs execution paths inside workflows. It classifies spend into productive and waste categories. It shows the evidence behind each finding. It puts confidence levels on savings estimates. It gives engineering a prioritized fix list.

Forensics depends on the layers below it.

If you have billing but no telemetry, you need observability before you need forensics.

If you have telemetry but no attribution, you need cost allocation before you need forensics.

If you have billing, telemetry, and attribution but still cannot explain why AI workflows cost what they cost, the missing layer is forensics.

Where Blackridge fits

Blackridge is layer 4.

It consumes observed or imported workflow evidence, pricing context, attribution, and runtime economics events, then produces forensic findings: waste classification, evidence chains, confidence-gated savings, and prioritized recommendations.

Your gateway stays.

Your observability platform stays.

Your FinOps dashboard stays.

Blackridge answers the question those tools leave open: why did this workflow cost what it did, and what should engineering fix first?
